Overview

My main research interest is in automated software testing, primarily targeting the JVM. As part of my PhD research, I am currently researching the idea to use biostatistical methods to quantify the performance of random fuzz testers. Specifically, my main hypothesis is that a fuzzer is more effective in finding bugs if it not only covers a lot of code, but also diversely exercises it â€” I call this concept behavioral diversity.

Inspired by the STADS framework, I propose to measure behavioral diversity based on the execution distribution of a fuzzer, analogous to how ecologists quantify biodiversity over the species distribution of an assemblage. To this end, I research to what extent a fuzzer that exhibits higher behavioral diversity inspires more confidence in the correctness of the program under test. Moreover, I have proposed BeDivFuzz, a coverage-guided fuzz testing technique that aims to diversely explore the program behaviors through an adaptive mutation strategy.
